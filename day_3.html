Phase 3: Intel Types

Professor's Briefing

Intel is not all the same, Coimbatore. A name is different from a bank account number. A blueprint is different from a security code. The computer must know what *type* of information it's handling. Today, you'll learn the three most basic data types: **strings** (text), **integers** (whole numbers), and **floats** (decimal numbers).

The Blueprint: Core Data Types

Strings (str)

A string is a sequence of characters, or simply, text. You must always enclose strings in quotes (`"` or `'`). Candidate names, job titles, and email addresses are all strings.

Integers (int)

An integer is a whole number, without a decimal point. The number of open positions, a candidate's years of experience, or an employee ID are typically integers.

Floats (float)

A float, or floating-point number, is a number that has a decimal point. A performance review score like `4.5`, a budget amount like `1500.75`, or a candidate's GPA are floats.

Why This Matters To You

This is critical. You can't do mathematics on a string. You can't send an email to an integer. Knowing the data type helps you understand the *constraints* and *capabilities* of the data a developer is working with. When they say they need to "cast a variable to an int," they mean they need to convert a piece of data (like "5") into a number (5) so they can perform calculations with it.
