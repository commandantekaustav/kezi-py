Phase 8: True or False

Professor's Briefing

Sometimes, the only intel you need is a simple "yes" or "no". Is the door locked? Is the guard asleep? In Python, we have a data type for this: the **boolean**. It has only two possible values: `True` or `False`. They are the foundation of all logic and decision-making in code.

The Blueprint: Booleans & Comparisons

Booleans are most often the result of a comparison. We use **comparison operators** to ask true-or-false questions about our data.

Comparison Operators

  • `==` : Equal to
  • `!=` : Not equal to
  • `>` : Greater than
  • `<` : Less than
  • `>=` : Greater than or equal to
  • `<=` : Less than or equal to

Code Workbench


interview_score = 92
years_experience = 5

# Let's ask some True/False questions.
is_high_scorer = interview_score > 90
print(f"Is the candidate a high scorer? {is_high_scorer}") # Output: True

is_senior_candidate = years_experience > 8
print(f"Is the candidate a senior? {is_senior_candidate}") # Output: False

is_perfect_score = interview_score == 100
print(f"Was it a perfect score? {is_perfect_score}") # Output: False

is_not_junior = years_experience != 1
print(f"Is the candidate not a junior? {is_not_junior}") # Output: True

# You can store booleans in variables directly.
offer_sent = False
background_check_passed = True
print(f"Has the offer been sent? {offer_sent}")

Why This Matters To You

Every decision a program makes boils down to a `True` or `False` question. "Is the user logged in?" (`True`). "Is the password correct?" (`True`). "Does the cart have items?" (`True`). When you understand this, you understand the binary heart of programming logic. It's the simple switch that controls the entire complex machine.
